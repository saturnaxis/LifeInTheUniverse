# Book FAQ

## How should I use this book?

Use this book as a guide to the main ideas in ASTR120. Read actively, pause at checkpoint questions, look carefully at figures, and use the examples to connect scientific ideas to evidence.

You do not need to memorize every detail. Focus on the larger questions:

- What claim is being made?
- What evidence supports the claim?
- What alternative explanations are possible?
- How does this idea connect to the search for life in the universe?

## Do I need to be good at math for this course?

No. This course uses basic quantitative reasoning, but it is not designed as a math-heavy course.

Most calculations will involve simple arithmetic, ratios, unit conversions, or interpreting graphs. These calculations are included because numbers help make astronomy more understandable. They let us estimate distances, timescales, sizes, temperatures, and probabilities.

The goal is not to become fast at calculation. The goal is to use numbers to support scientific reasoning.

## Do I need to know Python?

No. Python examples are included for students who are interested in exploring further, but they are not the main focus of the course.

When Python appears, it will usually repeat a simple calculation, create a graph, or show how changing one value affects another. You are welcome to run the code, change values, and experiment with it, but you are not expected to become a programmer.

## Why does this book include science and evidence?

Astronomy and astrobiology deal with big questions: How did the universe form? How did planets form? What makes Earth habitable? Could life exist elsewhere?

These questions are exciting, but science requires more than excitement. A scientific claim needs evidence. Evidence does not usually prove a claim with perfect certainty, but it can make a claim more or less likely.

Throughout this course, you should ask how we know something, not just what scientists currently think.

## Can I use AI tools while studying?

Yes. AI tools can be useful for studying when used responsibly.

Good uses of AI include:

- summarizing a difficult section,
- explaining vocabulary,
- generating practice questions,
- helping you compare ideas,
- checking whether your explanation is clear,
- or helping you study from the notes.

Poor uses of AI include:

- copying an answer without understanding it,
- treating AI as always correct,
- using AI instead of reading the notes,
- or submitting AI-generated work as your own thinking.

AI should help you learn. It should not replace your learning.

## How should I write a good AI prompt?

A good AI prompt usually includes a persona, context, and task.

For example:

> Act as a patient introductory astronomy tutor. I am an ASTR120 student learning about exoplanets for the first time. Explain how the transit method works, describe what evidence astronomers measure, and give me three questions to check my understanding.

The persona is “a patient introductory astronomy tutor.”  
The context is “I am an ASTR120 student learning about exoplanets for the first time.”  
The task is “Explain how the transit method works, describe what evidence astronomers measure, and give me three questions.”

This structure usually gives better results than a short prompt like “Explain transits.”

## Can I use NotebookLM with these notes?

Yes. NotebookLM is encouraged as a study-support tool for this course.

A good workflow is:

1. Add the relevant section or chapter of the course notes as a source.
2. Ask NotebookLM for a short summary.
3. Read the section yourself.
4. Ask NotebookLM to generate review questions.
5. Try answering the questions before looking at any suggested answers.
6. Return to the notes to check your understanding.

NotebookLM is most useful when it helps you interact with the notes more deeply.

## What if an AI explanation disagrees with the notes?

Rely on the course notes, class discussion, and reliable scientific sources first. AI tools can produce incorrect or misleading answers.

If something does not match, ask a follow-up question such as:

> This explanation seems different from my course notes. Compare the two explanations and identify what might be inconsistent.

Then bring the question to class, office hours, or your instructor.

## What if I get stuck?

Start by identifying what kind of confusion you have.

Are you confused by:

- a vocabulary word?
- a figure?
- a scientific claim?
- the evidence for a claim?
- a calculation?
- a comparison between two ideas?

Once you know where you are stuck, ask a more focused question. You can ask your instructor, classmates, NotebookLM, or another AI tool, but the goal should be the same: improve your own understanding.
